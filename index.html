What is Land Use Management?
Land use management is the art of managing, directing, administrating, regulating and controlling the use of land by government for sustainable urban development.
Land-use management can also be defined as the process of regulating the use of land by a central authority. Usually, this is done in an effort to promote a more desirable social and environmental outcomes as well as a more efficient use of resources.
without further ado, let's discuss the objectives of land use managament
The objectives of land use management can be broadly classified into 4 key areas which include:
  • Economic
  • Environmental
  • Social
  • Political

Land Use Management Practices from the Perspective of China
-As a result of the growth spurt in China as well as the challenges that came with it in the 1990s, the chinese government came up with strict policies to guide and manage the use of land. One of which is the "Link Policy"

Link Policy

- The link policy was introduced to ink the increase in urban construction land with the decrease in rural construction land.

- The policy called for relocating farmers who lived in spacious, scattered, and poorly-provisioned settlements into modern, concentrated communities, while converting their former homestead land into farmland.

Components of the Link Policy
  • Compulsory Land Acquisition Program
  • Displacement and Resettlement Program
  • Transferable Development Rights Program

Programs Explained...

  • Compulsory land acquisition is the right and action of government to take property not owned by it, for poblic use. This right is also known as entitlement domain and the action is known as condemnation

  • As the name implies, people could be resettled from their original locations for the greater good, in order to ensure efficient use of land. There are two types of displacement and resettlement programs widely adopted in China. These includes: Development-induced and the Environment-induced displacement program

  • A Transferable Development program (TDR) is a market based solution that provides for development rights to be exchanged between wllling sellers and buyers in a free-market system. A TDR program provides a new planning instrument that redirects development rather than simply prohibiting development.
Land Use Management Practices from the Nigerian Perspective
  • Formal land use planning and management practices in Nigeria began in 1863 with the enactment of the Town Improvement Ordinance by the colonial government. This was to control development and urban sanitation in Lagos.

  • However, modern planning could be said to begin in the country in 1946 when the Nigerian Town and Country Ordinance was enacted
  • In Nigeria today, one of the approaches that has been adopted to manage the use of land is the preparation of comprehensive broad land use plan, to contol development of a long period of time. However, field investigation by Aribigbola (2006) revealed that most of what was implemented in the land use pan was not implemented.

  • As a result of the shortcomings of the master plans, the layout plans evolved, which should be in sync to the master plan and approved before any development takes place. If the plan falls short, it should be returned for correction to ensure its meet all standards and requirements.

Land Use Management Practices from the Perspective of the United Kingdom
  • The three biggest land uses in England are agriculture at 63 per cent, forest, open land and water at 21 per cent and residential gardens at 5 per cent.

  • While agriculture comprises a small economic sector, it is the existing land use for 71% of UK land. This resource is worth hundreds of £billions in current market value, and this under-represents the societal value of land in providing a multitude of ecosystem services.

  • The UK has existing environmental and cultural heritage obligations and objectives which require significant investment in land management.

The UK Government's Approach to Land use Management


The UK Government’s explicit long-term plan is to transition environmental policy and agricultural policy to a ‘polluter pays’ model as the foundation of our future environmental land management system.

  • In January 2018, the UK Government published a 25 year Environmental Plan, which was meant to improve the way they manage and incentivise land management, including designing and delivering a new environmental land management system. The new approach will recognize good practices that build up and bolster natural and heritage assets


  • It will also take account of the negative effects of a range of land uses and activities. It will require a balance of incentives and regulations influencing decisions so that they use land in a way that supports cost-effective, sustainable growth

  • From 2022 onwards, a new environmental land management system will be the cornerstone of the agricultural policy in the UK, It will consist of a new scheme that pays providers for delivering environmentally beneficial outcomes; and will provide support for farmers and land managers as they move towards a more effective application of the ‘polluter pays’ principle. As soon as it’s practical, they propose to maintain a strong regulatory baseline of standards that reflects the ‘polluter pays’ principle.

  • The new environmental land management system will be underpinned by natural capital principles, so that the benefits the natural environment provides for people and wildlife are properly valued and used to inform decisions on future land management

  • The new system aims to deliver benefits such as improved air, water and soil quality; increased biodiversity; climate change mitigation and adaptation; and cultural benefits that improve their mental and physical well-being, while protecting our historic environment.
The new Environmental Land Management System involves all of the following

  1. Enhanced support and continued funding for technical advice for projects which meet national priorities and require complex, places-specific management such as wetland and woodland creation, or peatland restoration.

  2. Funding for collaborative projects, incentivising land managers to work together to secure environmental improvements at landscape and catchment level, supporting co-ordinated action on areas such as diffuse water pollution and the protection of priority habitats.

  3. Capital grants: supporting land managers to adopt sustainable practices and reduce negative environmental impacts in a transition towards a fuller application of the ‘polluter pays’ principle.

  4. The transition from area-based payments, which comprise the majority of payments to farmers under (Pillar 1) of the EU’s Common Agricultural Policy, to a system of ‘public money for public goods’ is hugely significant. It has the potential to transform the way the countryside looks, as well as how (and whether) they deploy terms such as ‘subsidy’ and ‘support’ in the context of new legal contracts for providing goods that the public purchase.
Land Use Management Practices from the Perspective of Brazil

  • Brazil has one of the greatest spans of tropical forest in the world (about 64 percent of tropical forest), resulting in huge amounts of timber potential for burning and wood processing

  • Even with that, Land use in Brazil is currently unmanaged and does not meet current global environmental sustainability targets, which are set to be met by 2030.

  • Also, Poverty is rife in some parts of Brazil which results in land use issues from the rural sectors where poorer families need to be self-sufficient when providing their own food.

Due to the nature of land ownership in Brazil being predominantly private, efficiently allocating land becomes a tedious task. This made the government come up a Land use Policy Assessment (LUPA)

  • A Land Use Policy Assessment (LUPA) is an effort that aims to explain land use policies and recommend improvements and redirections of land ownership and use based on variables that would boost land use efficiency and offer the greatest economic advantages.


  • A LUPA can have a direct impact on poorer communities, as they may not be able to make the modifications on their own property

  • Agricultural intensification systems, for example, are implemented as part of a LUPA, adopting a crop system that favors intensive agriculture in the area currently available, rather than deforestation, which would create more room for agriculture.
